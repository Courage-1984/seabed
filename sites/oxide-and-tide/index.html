The Anvil and the Abyss

Forging Yesterday’s Shipwrecks into Tomorrow’s Armour

When a classic commercial helmet arrives at our Orkney workshop, its greatest vulnerabilities are hidden within the threaded neck rings and Braithwaite viewports. To repair these high-stress areas without introducing modern structural anomalies, we look to the sea itself. We harvest copper sheeting from localized early 20th-century blockships sunk right here in the flow. Decades under heavy coastal currents have naturally stress-relieved this metal, imbuing the alloy with an exceptional structural ductility that modern mills simply cannot replicate.

Every patch is carefully hammered out on an 1890 Peter Wright anvil, shaped to match the unique curvature of the original copper shell. We hand-braze the components using high-silver solder, forming a molecular bond that stands up to immense structural pressure. This is followed by an intricate hand-pouring of bespoke bio-resin gaskets, replacing deteriorated vulcanised rubber with an invisible, marine-stable barrier. The result is a seamless structural repair that honors the patina of the past while providing absolute security at depth.

Workshop Manifest

Apparatus Under Structural Overhaul

We possess deep structural familiarity with the standard configurations that define early 20th-century deep-water salvage. Our Orkney workshop routinely accepts and reconstructs Siebe Gorman 6-bolt systems, restoring the critical clearances within their heavy threaded collars. We regularly process US Navy Mark V setups, replacing depleted tinned interiors while maintaining exact factory weight tolerances. Early Heinke apparatus—often suffering from brittle spitcocks and compromised non-return valves—are carefully stripped and reinforced using period-correct brazing techniques. Understanding the precise nuances and operational idiosyncrasies of these varying manufacturer specifications is essential; a failure to respect the original pressure mathematics compromises both the artifact's historical integrity and its functional survival beneath the waves.

The Restoration Cycle

A Meticulous Timeline

The repair of a deep-submergence artifact follows a strict, predictable structural path; precision cannot be rushed. The cycle begins with ultrasonic cleaning baths, systematically stripping decades of embedded salt and marine calcification without scoring the underlying bronze. Once stabilised, we conduct exhaustive thickness testing across the breastplate and bonnet, mapping areas of severe galvanic corrosion. Where the copper has grown dangerously thin or brittle, we implement controlled annealing—raising the metal temperature to exactly 650 degrees Celsius to restore ductility before hot-working replacement patches. The final stage involves the careful tin-plating of the interior, ensuring no toxic copper salts can accumulate within the breathing atmosphere. Every step is documented, providing a complete metallurgical history of the restoration.

Tidal Inquiries

Shipping Heavy Freight

Transporting a forty-kilogram brass and copper diving helmet to the Orkney islands requires specialised logistics and secure packaging. We mandate that all incoming apparatus be bolted into custom wooden crating, braced with high-density foam to prevent shifting during transit. Do not ship helmets in standard cardboard freight boxes; the concentrated weight of the breastplate will cause critical transit damage. We coordinate directly with maritime couriers who service the northern island routes, ensuring your historical equipment is tracked and insured from the mainland to our Scapa Flow smithy. For specific weight specifications and crate lining guidelines, please contact us prior to dispatch.
