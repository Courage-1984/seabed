The 1920s Naval Aneroid Refit

Our flagship offering is the 1920s Naval Aneroid Refit, a heavy-duty weather instrument originally forged for the brutal conditions of the South Atlantic. We source these heavy brass bulkheads from decommissioned maritime salvage yards across the coast. Once in the workshop, the six-inch solid brass casing is stripped of salt corrosion, hand-polished, and sealed with a micro-crystalline wax to prevent tarnishing in humid environments.

The internal mechanical linkages, often seized by decades of sea salt, are ultrasonically cleaned and lubricated with aerospace-grade synthetic oils that will not freeze at high altitudes or degrade in the intense South African summer heat. We replace the standard glass with shatter-resistant bevelled crystal, ensuring the instrument can survive the vibrations of a 4x4 vehicle traversing unpaved farm roads. It is the ultimate off-grid predictive tool.

Mechanical Inquiries

Do I need to adjust the instrument for my specific altitude when it arrives?

No. During the purchasing process, we require your exact farm or location altitude in metres above sea level. We mechanically recalibrate the beryllium-copper leaf springs to that specific baseline before dispatch. When you unbox the instrument, it will immediately register accurate atmospheric pressure for your precise elevation without any further manual adjustment.

How do I read a dropping needle versus a rising one?

A rapidly dropping needle indicates a sudden decrease in atmospheric pressure, which mechanically signifies an approaching severe weather system, such as a severe Highveld thunderstorm or high winds. A steadily rising needle indicates increasing pressure, pointing toward clearing skies and stable, dry weather.

Does the glass face fog up in high humidity?

No. During our laboratory refit sequence, we replace any degraded standard glass with shatter-resistant bevelled crystal. Furthermore, we seal the internal brass casing using micro-crystalline wax and high-grade maritime gaskets, effectively preventing internal condensation even during intense, humid coastal weather.

How often does the instrument require recalibration?

Unlike digital sensors that degrade within years, a properly restored mechanical aneroid barometer is designed for multi-generational use. However, to maintain absolute scientific accuracy across extreme temperature fluctuations, we recommend returning the instrument for a preventative ultrasonic bath and tension diagnostic every ten to twelve years.

What happens if the instrument is dropped?

While our shatter-resistant crystal and heavy brass bulkheads provide immense exterior protection, severe kinetic shock can jolt the microscopic internal hairsprings out of alignment. If this occurs, do not attempt to open the casing. Contact the workshop immediately to arrange a secure courier collection for a diagnostic recalibration.
