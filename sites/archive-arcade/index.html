Mechanical Memories Restored Properly

Authentic analogue photo booths restored for collectors, venues and people who still appreciate waiting for a printed photograph.

Archive Arcade began with a neglected Japanese photo booth that most buyers considered beyond saving. Rather than stripping it for parts, our workshop rebuilt every moving assembly, documented every repair and proved that analogue photography still has a place in modern public spaces. Today we restore rare mechanical booths for museums, independent cinemas, universities, photographers and cultural venues across Britain, combining careful conservation with practical upgrades that allow each machine to work safely for many more years.

Featured Restorations

Circa 1988 • Japan

The Mori PX-88 Heritage Booth

The Mori PX-88 remains the workshop's most requested restoration because it balances robust engineering with an unmistakably late-1980s industrial design. Each example receives a complete mechanical rebuild, replacement drive belts manufactured to original dimensions, fresh seals, renewed lighting assemblies and discreet electrical protection that satisfies current expectations without altering the appearance of the cabinet. Buyers receive a printed maintenance journal documenting every stage of restoration, recommended servicing intervals and replacement part references. Rather than treating each machine as an anonymous product, Archive Arcade records its individual history so future custodians understand precisely how it was preserved and what work has been completed over time.

Circa 1974 • Germany

Auto-Reflex Model IV

Rescued from a decommissioned seaside amusement arcade in Margate, the Auto-Reflex Model IV represents the pinnacle of mid-seventies European electromechanical engineering. This particular unit required three months of intensive brass fabrication to recreate missing coin-handling mechanisms. We retained the original textured laminate exterior, matching period-correct veneers where water damage had compromised the base. The chemical delivery pump system was entirely re-piped with modern, chemical-resistant silicone tubing to prevent future blockages. It now resides in a private collection in Manchester, capturing striking, high-contrast monochrome strips on authentic archival paper.

Circa 1995 • UK

Studio-O-Matic 2000

A rare British-built machine originally designed for passport photography, the Studio-O-Matic 2000 is renowned for its incredibly sharp optics and reliable dual-flash strobe system. When we acquired this cabinet from a defunct post office in Leeds, the internal timing cams were seized solid. Following a total teardown, we machined replacement nylon cams on our workshop lathe and upgraded the safety relays to modern specifications. The heavy steel enclosure was stripped back and finished in its original factory hammered-grey enamel. It now operates dependably in a busy London independent cinema.

Why Printed Photographs Still Matter

A printed photograph demands patience. There is no endless scrolling, no duplicate gallery and no instant deletion. The short wait while a strip develops becomes part of the experience, encouraging conversation rather than distraction. That simple difference explains why restored photo booths continue to attract queues at galleries, independent cinemas and design festivals. Visitors remember the sound of the mechanism, the movement of the curtain and the moment the finished strip finally appears. Archive Arcade restores more than machinery; it preserves a ritual that digital photography quietly left behind. Every installation is intended to be used, maintained and enjoyed for years rather than admired as a static exhibit.

Patience Chemistry Permanence

The Nine-Bench Revival Method

Every machine that enters our workshop follows the same structured restoration process because consistency matters far more than speed. We begin by recording every panel, mechanism and electrical assembly before dismantling the cabinet. Corrosion is treated rather than hidden, replacement parts are fabricated only where originals cannot reasonably be repaired, and period finishes are matched against surviving paint beneath protected panels. The optical path is recalibrated, rollers are rebuilt, gears are cleaned by hand and every fastener is catalogued before reassembly. Only after repeated testing with photographic paper and chemistry do we complete modern electrical safety upgrades. The result is not a machine that merely resembles its former self, but one that retains its character while being dependable enough for daily public use.

1. Assessment & Documentation

We photograph and catalogue every component before a single screw is turned. A detailed condition report notes all structural weaknesses and missing elements, forming the blueprint for the entire eight-week restoration schedule.

2. Structural Preservation

Cabinets are carefully stripped. Rot is excised and replaced with matching timber, while steel frames are treated for oxidation. We prioritise stabilising original materials over simply replacing them with modern equivalents.

3. Mechanical Rebuilding

The heart of the booth. Gearboxes are opened, ultrasonically cleaned, and repacked with correct-viscosity lubricants. Worn drive belts are replaced with precision-matched industrial bands to ensure timing remains flawless.

4. Optical Calibration

Lenses are disassembled, cleaned of fungus, and re-seated. Flash tubes and timing circuits are tested against light meters to guarantee consistent exposure across the entire four-frame photographic strip.

5. Electrical Upgrades

Original aesthetics are maintained while obsolete, unsafe wiring is entirely replaced. We install modern RCD protection, shielded cables, and PAT-tested components to meet strict contemporary venue safety regulations.

6. Wet-Testing & Commissioning

The machine undergoes a rigorous two-week testing phase running actual chemistry and paper. Only when we have achieved consistently perfect development and fixing do we declare the restoration complete and sign the logbook.
